YOUR CADDIE KNOWS YOUR GAME

A great caddie remembers your game — your tendencies, your distances — so you never re-explain yourself on the next hole. Memory is your caddie. Have one conversation to set it up and every future chat already knows who you are and what you're focused on. Claude (per-project + across chats) and Copilot both remember; Gemini's is lighter today.

The memory setup conversation — copy, edit, send oncePlease remember this about me for future chats: I'm [NAME], [ROLE] on the [TEAM / DEPARTMENT] at TaylorMade. I focus on [what you own — projects, areas, stakeholders]. My tone should be [clear, concise, professional]. When I ask for work, default to my context, match that tone, and flag where you need a specific detail from me. Tell me what you've saved.

What to put in memory

  • Who you are and your team.
  • What you own — projects, areas, stakeholders.
  • Your tone and any always-rules.
  • Formats you ask for often — briefs, reports, recaps.

Why it's worth 5 minutes

  • Move 1 of the Pre-Shot Routine gets shorter — you skip the intro every time.
  • Outputs start in your context, not generic.
  • Update it any time your focus changes.
  • It stays in your account, inside TaylorMade's environment.

The Range · the one habit that changes everything

THE PRE-SHOT ROUTINE

Every good golfer runs the same routine before every swing — read the lie, pick the club, see the shot. Great prompting is the same discipline. Three moves, every time, and vague requests become expert-level outputs. Set up memory once — your caddie, back in The Bag — and Move 1 gets shorter every swing.

Move 1

Read the lie

The situation today.

Give the AI what's specific to this shot — the product, the market, the deadline. If your caddie (memory) already knows who you are, skip the intro and go straight to today's context.

Move 2

Pick the club

Define the task.

Say exactly what you want it to do: "Draft the project brief," "Summarize this report," "Compare these two options." One clear shot, not "help me with this."

Move 3

See the shot

Specify the rules.

Picture the landing: format, length, tone, audience. "One-page exec brief, premium voice, ready for HQ." Now it lands where you aimed.

All three moves, in one breathYou are a senior brand marketer for TaylorMade Golf launching the P-Series irons in Korea this season — precision-focused golfers, heavy KakaoTalk usage, Callaway pushing fitting bundles. Draft three campaign messages and a one-page launch brief. Premium, confident voice, ready for HQ review.
And the 4th move — commit & adjust

First answer 80% there? That's normal — it's a conversation, not a vending machine. Take a mulligan: "tighten to the top 3, add a Japan angle, make it punchier." You refine, you don't restart. (Five parts — role, context, task, format, specifics — the difference between a vague ask and a great answer.)

Going Pro · turn one good round into a system

FROM A CHAT TO A SYSTEM

An amateur hits one great shot and hopes to repeat it. A pro builds a swing they can reproduce on demand. Same with AI: most people live in one-off chats. Going pro means climbing three rungs — and each one is genuinely easy.

Rung 1 · where every idea starts

A Chat

Fast, disposable, perfect for exploring. But when it ends, the context is gone — and you re-explain yourself next time.

Rung 2 · when a topic keeps coming back

A Project

A workspace with standing instructions + your files, so every chat inside it already knows the context. New Project → paste your instructions → drop in your files. Two minutes. A recurring project runs this way — a "read-this-first" instructions file with the key documents right beside it, so every chat starts in context.

Rung 3 · when a task repeats the same way

A Skill

Package the how-to once and Claude runs it on command — in any chat, not just one project — and you can hand it to the whole team. That weekly report you rebuild by hand becomes exactly this: a skill Claude runs on command, that you hand to the whole team.

Every platform has its own version of "rung 3"

The ladder is clearest in Claude (Projects + Skills), but each tool has its own repeatable layer: Copilot has agents — Researcher, Analyst, PowerPoint — and Gemini Enterprise has NotebookLM, Deep Research & Idea Generation. Build the repeatable thing once; hand it to the team.

Going Pro · 1 · feed it your assets

IT BUILDS WITH WHAT IT CAN SEE

Your AI tools build real things — a brief, a deck, a one-pager, a working tracker, an on-brand graphic (Claude calls them artifacts) — but they can only use what you put in their hands. Give them the real materials and the output stops being a generic guess.

Hand it the real thing

  • Attach the file, or open a connector to where it already lives — SharePoint, Box.
  • Point it at the real source — the data, the deck, the examples — so it builds from fact, not a guess.
  • The packaging project works because the die templates and the spec sit right next to the work.

Markdown — the brief that builds it

  • Shape the scope and content in a chat or a project — argue it out, get it right.
  • Then have Claude write it as a clean .md brief: compact, structured, token-light.
  • That brief is what you hand to Claude Design to build the artifact — fast, no re-explaining.
⛳ The pro move

Think in chat, decide in a project, then output a tight .md brief — the scope and the content. Hand that to Claude Design and it builds the piece. The brief is the bridge from idea to artifact.

Going Pro · 2 · on-brand by default

DESIGN THAT LOOKS LIKE US

Hand Claude Design your .md brief and it turns it into polished visuals — decks, one-pagers, mockups — exporting to PowerPoint, PDF, or Canva for the team to finish. You bring the scope; it brings the craft.

On-brand, automatically

  • Brand isn't a guess — the TaylorMade brand-guide skill already holds our voice, colors, type, and logo rules, so design starts on-brand, not generic.
  • Comment on any element, nudge spacing or color, and it applies the fix across the whole piece.
  • For photoreal product imagery, still reach for Gemini Enterprise or Adobe Firefly — those generate images; Claude Design lays out the design.

Build once, share with the team

  • A strong prompt, project, or design system is worth far more shared than hoarded.
  • Package the repeatable ones as skills — the brand guide itself can be a skill every region runs.
  • The multiplier: one person builds it, the whole team plays better.
Claude Design is in beta (Pro · Max · Team · Enterprise) and default-off for Enterprise — IT flips it on, then sets up our design system once so everything inherits TaylorMade's colors, type, and components. For finished, photoreal imagery, the approved tools stay Gemini Enterprise & Adobe Firefly.

Going Pro · 3 · trust, but verify

SIGN YOUR OWN SCORECARD

In golf you sign your own card — get it wrong and it's on you. Same here: AI gets you 80% of the way fast, but you're the player of record. Pros build the check into the routine.

make it show its work

Ask for sources

"Where did each number come from?" "Cite the doc and the line." If it can't point to a source, treat it as a draft, not a fact.

pressure-test it

Have it check itself

"Argue the other side." "What would a skeptic challenge?" "What did you assume?" A second pass catches what a first draft misses.

you sign it

Own the last 20%

Numbers, names, claims, tone — your judgment is the value. Never forward what you haven't read.

⛳ The bar

Before it leaves your hands: would this run in front of leadership, unedited? If not, take one more swing. Accuracy is the whole game.
